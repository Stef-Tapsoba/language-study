# B1 Language Coverage Guide
*French, Spanish, Italian, Japanese, Korean*

---

## What B1 Means (Universal Definition)

At **B1 (Intermediate)** level, a learner can:

- Hold a sustained conversation on familiar and general topics (10–15 minutes)
- Express and explain opinions, beliefs, and reactions
- Understand the main points of standard speech on familiar subjects
- Read straightforward texts such as news articles, blog posts, and letters
- Write connected paragraphs with cohesive structure
- Deal with most travel situations and unexpected complications

B1 marks the shift from **memorised phrases** to **generating original ideas** in the language. This is the most cognitively demanding transition in the CEFR scale.

---

## Romance Languages: French, Spanish, Italian

### Grammar Coverage
- Full tense system in active use:
  - Perfect (completed events)
  - Imperfect (background, habitual, interrupted actions)
  - Simple future (promises, predictions, formal plans)
  - Conditional (polite requests, hypotheticals, reported speech)
- Relative clauses with all pronouns (dont / cuyo, où / donde, etc.)
- Si-clause hypotheticals — present real condition (si + présent → futur)
- Introductory subjunctive: verbs of wanting, emotion, doubt (French: que + subjonctif; Spanish: que + presente subjuntivo)
- Combined and double object pronouns
- Passive voice (introductory recognition)

### Core Vocabulary Themes
- Society, environment, technology
- Work and career
- Travel and cross-cultural experience
- Media and current events
- Abstract concepts: opinion, possibility, necessity, change

### Collocations and Idioms
B1 is where learners first encounter the **collocation gap** — understanding every word in a phrase but not knowing that native speakers use them together as a fixed chunk. Examples: *prendre une décision* (not *faire*), *echar de menos* (not *perder*), *rendersi conto* (not *capire*). Common light idioms and fixed expressions also begin appearing: *coûter les yeux de la tête*, *estar en las nubes*, *non ci piove*. Learners acquire these as vocabulary items, not as a grammar category.

### Functional Skills
- Narrate a complex past event using both perfect and imperfect
- Express a hypothesis ("if I had time, I would…")
- Agree, disagree, and qualify statements
- Explain advantages and disadvantages
- Write a structured email or informal essay

### Language-Specific Notes
- **French**: imparfait vs passé composé contrast reaches full competence; subjonctif in fixed structures (il faut que, je veux que); conditionnel de politesse (je voudrais) becomes natural
- **Spanish**: pretérito indefinido / imperfecto distinction mastered; present subjunctive activates a large class of clauses; por vs para distinctions clarified
- **Italian**: imperfetto / passato prossimo contrast as in Spanish; condizionale activates polite register; subjunctive exposure begins (congiuntivo presente)

## Japanese (B1 / JLPT N3)

### Writing System Progress
- ~300–500 kanji in reading (daily vocabulary: 仕事, 家族, 社会, 問題)
- Target-language explanations use kanji freely
- Learner reads most common signs, labels, and simple articles

### Grammar Coverage
- Obligation and necessity: ~なければならない / ~なくてはいけない
- Prohibition with nuance: ~てはいけない vs ~てはだめ
- Change over time: ~ようになる (have come to, now able to)
- Listing varied actions: ~たり~たりする (do things like … and …)
- Explanatory tone: ~んです / ~のです (giving reasons, seeking explanation)
- Expressing opinions: ~と思います、~と思っている
- Clause linking: ~ので (because — formal), ~のに (despite), ~けど (but — softer)
- Volitional form: ~しよう / ~しましょう
- Conditional: ~ば (if — formal/literary), ~と (if — habitual), ~たら (if/when)
- Passive: ~られる (plain) / ~られます (polite)

### Sentence Structure
- Complex multi-clause sentences with varied connectors
- Modifying clauses (noun-modifying verb phrases placed before the noun)
- Growing natural use of particle は for contrast vs が for new information

### Collocations and Idioms
At B1, learners begin encountering set verb-noun pairings that must be memorised as chunks: *お世話になる* (to be in someone's care / thank you for your help), *気になる* (to be on one's mind), *気をつける* (to be careful). These resist word-for-word translation and are acquired as fixed expressions. Four-character idioms (四字熟語) such as *一石二鳥* (two birds, one stone) appear in reading at B1 but are not yet expected in productive use.

### Core Vocabulary Themes
- Society and environment (社会、環境、問題)
- Health and lifestyle (健康、生活、習慣)
- Work and relationships (仕事、関係、チーム)
- Media, news, and current events
- Abstract nouns: opinion, change, possibility

### Functional Skills
- Explain reasoning with nuance (~ので、~から、~ため)
- Describe changes and trends (~ようになる、~くなる)
- Express obligation, permission, and prohibition
- Retell a story or summarise a text
- Write a paragraph-length opinion

### Common Challenges
- Three conditionals (ば、たら、と) — choosing the appropriate one by context
- Passive used both for grammar and to express suffering/inconvenience (迷惑の受身)
- Noun-modification clauses (entire verb phrases before nouns) require SOV fluency

## Korean (B1 / TOPIK II-3)

### Writing System Progress
- Hangul fully fluent; focus is entirely on grammar and vocabulary depth

### Grammar Coverage
- Conditionals: -(으)면 (if/when — general), -았/었으면 (if it were the case that)
- Concession: -지만 (but/although), -(으)면서 (while/even though)
- Causal: -기 때문에 (because — formal/objective), -아/어서 (because — sequential/causal)
- Attempt/try: -아/어 보다 (try doing)
- Purpose: -(으)러 가다/오다 (go/come to do)
- Obligation: -아/어야 하다 / 되다 (must/have to)
- Reported speech: -다고 하다 / -라고 하다 (they say that…)
- Nominalisation: -기 (verb → noun: 먹기가 어렵다) and -는 것 (the fact of doing)
- Passive and causative forms (introductory: 보이다, 들리다)

### Sentence Structure
- Fluent use of connected clauses with varied connectors
- Reporting and quoting other speech (-다고 해요, -냐고 해요)
- Nominalised verbs as sentence subjects and objects

### Collocations and Idioms
Korean B1 learners begin recognising fixed verb-noun collocations that cannot be assembled from individual word meanings: *결정을 내리다* (make a decision — not *하다*), *관심을 갖다* (take an interest), *영향을 미치다* (have an influence). Common fixed expressions also appear: *어쩔 수 없다* (can't be helped), *별로예요* (not really / not great). These are treated as vocabulary items. Proverbs (속담) appear in reading at B1 — *가는 말이 고와야 오는 말이 곱다* (treat others as you wish to be treated) — but are recognised rather than produced.

### Core Vocabulary Themes
- Society and culture (사회, 문화, 환경)
- Work, education, and career (직업, 교육, 취업)
- Health and wellbeing (건강, 스트레스, 운동)
- News and current events
- Abstract vocabulary: reason, opinion, change, result

### Functional Skills
- Express conditions and hypotheticals
- Report what others said or asked
- Give reasons using formal and informal connectors
- Debate a simple position with supporting reasons
- Write a coherent multi-paragraph argument

### Common Challenges
- Distinguishing -기 때문에 (formal cause) from -아/어서 (general cause) — register awareness
- Reported speech grammar (tense changes inside reported clauses)
- Passive vs active voice — Korean passives are lexically derived (보다 → 보이다), not regular

## Register and Speech Styles

At B1, register switching is **deliberate and rule-governed** — learners know the social rules and apply them consciously rather than defaulting to one form. A key new skill is understanding the **social cost of register mismatch**: sounding too formal reads as cold or distant; sounding too informal reads as rude or over-familiar.

### Romance Languages
B1 learners navigate formal and informal registers confidently and begin using **colloquial spoken language** alongside formal equivalents. They are aware that written language (emails, essays) uses different structures than spoken conversation.

| Register | When to use |
|---|---|
| Formal (*vous / usted / Lei*) | Professional settings, official correspondence, older strangers, authority figures |
| Informal (*tu / tú / tu*) | Friends, family, peers in casual settings |
| Colloquial | Casual spoken conversation with close peers — contractions, short sentences, informal vocabulary |

The **conditionnel de politesse** (je voudrais, je pourrais) is a key B1 tool: using the conditional instead of the present makes a request sound polite and non-imposing even with *tu*. This is equivalent to "I would like" vs "I want" in English.

### Japanese
B1 learners have **teineigo (丁寧語 / です/ます) fully automatic** and **casual plain form confident** with close peers. The new register awareness at B1 is understanding that **keigo (honorific language)** exists and is obligatory in specific contexts — even if production is not yet expected.

| Register | When to use |
|---|---|
| Teineigo (です/ます) | Default for most adult interactions, new acquaintances, service, colleagues |
| Plain form | Close friends, family, peers of similar or younger age; casual text/social media |
| Keigo (recognition) | Business settings, speaking to customers/superiors/elders — learner recognises it and understands its function |

**Key B1 awareness**: Switching accidentally into plain form with someone in a superior position is a noticeable social error. Learners at B1 have the self-monitoring to avoid this.

### Korean
B1 learners command the **full register matrix** and understand when each level is socially appropriate:

| Register | Form | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| 합쇼체 (formal polite) | -습니다/-ㅂ니다 | Presentations, announcements, job interviews, formal public speech |
| 해요체 (informal polite) | -아요/-어요 | Everyday polite default — strangers, colleagues, older people, service |
| 해체 (plain/informal) | Plain verb endings | Close friends, younger people, casual written messages — same as 반말 |
| 반말 | No -요 | Close peers, younger siblings, children |

**Key B1 rule**: Using -기 때문에 (formal cause connector) vs -아/어서 (general cause) is itself a register signal — -기 때문에 in casual speech sounds stiff; -아/어서 in formal writing sounds too casual. B1 learners start tracking this.

## Comparative Overview

| Feature | Romance Languages | Japanese | Korean |
|------|------------------|----------|--------|
| Defining B1 Grammar | Tense mastery + introductory subjunctive | Conditionals + passive + explanatory ~んです | Conditionals + reported speech + nominalisation |
| Past Tense Complexity | Two-tense contrast (perfect vs imperfect) | Aspect system via te-form | Single past + aspect via connector choice |
| New Script Demands | None | ~300–500 kanji | None |
| Biggest B1 Challenge | Subjunctive — new grammatical mood | Three conditional forms + passive nuance | Reported speech + formal/informal connector register |
| Productive Leap | Arguing and hypothesising | Explaining reasoning and nuance | Reporting and connecting ideas formally |

---

## Summary

- B1 is where learners become conversationally independent — they can handle most real-world interactions without preparation
- The subjunctive (Romance) and conditional forms (Japanese, Korean) are the primary grammatical hurdles
- Vocabulary at B1 shifts from concrete nouns to abstract concepts: society, opinion, change, possibility
- Reading authentic short texts (news summaries, blog posts) becomes accessible
- The A2→B1 jump is the largest cognitive leap in the CEFR scale — phrase recall gives way to generative language production
