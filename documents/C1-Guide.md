# C1 Language Coverage Guide
*French, Spanish, Italian, Japanese, Korean*

---

## What C1 Means (Universal Definition)

At **C1 (Advanced)** level, a learner can:

- Express themselves fluently, spontaneously, and precisely with little obvious searching for words
- Use language flexibly for social, academic, and professional purposes
- Produce clear, well-structured, detailed text on complex subjects with appropriate style
- Understand long, demanding texts and implicit meaning
- Understand extended speech at natural speed even when not clearly structured
- Grasp connotation, register, irony, and cultural subtext

C1 represents **near-professional proficiency** — the learner operates almost indistinguishably from a fluent non-native speaker in most contexts.

---

## Romance Languages: French, Spanish, Italian

### Grammar Coverage
- Complete tense and mood system in full productive use:
  - Subjunctive in all tenses (present, past, perfect, pluperfect) including literary registers
  - Conditional perfect ("would have done") in complex hypotheticals and reported speech
  - Sequence of tenses across reported speech, subordinate clauses, and embedded questions
- Passive, causative, and impersonal constructions used stylistically
- Nominalisations and clause compression for formal prose
- Ellipsis, pro-drop, and cohesion across long texts
- Discourse structuring: argumentation, digression, reformulation
- Idiomatic competence: fixed expressions, collocations, phrasal verbs (Italian: verbi fraseologici)

### Register and Style
- Full control of formal academic, professional, journalistic, and literary registers
- Stylistic variation: ability to adjust formality, tone, and vocabulary density deliberately
- Understanding of sociolinguistic variation — regional vocabulary, social dialects, historical registers (formal French tu/vous; Spanish vosotros vs ustedes; Italian Lei)

### Core Vocabulary Themes
- Philosophy, ethics, and abstract reasoning
- Literature, art, and cultural heritage
- Law, governance, and civic life
- Advanced science, technology, and research
- Proverbs, idioms, wordplay, and cultural reference

### Functional Skills
- Deliver structured, impromptu arguments on complex topics
- Write academic essays, formal reports, and analytical commentary
- Understand literature, film, and broadcast media fully
- Negotiate complex professional situations
- Appreciate humour, irony, and rhetorical devices in the target language

### Language-Specific Notes
- **French**: literary register (subjonctif imparfait, passé simple) distinguishes B2 from C1; C1 learners read Proust and de Beauvoir without a dictionary; accent and liaison mastery eliminates non-native markers from speech
- **Spanish**: full vosotros/ustedes code-switching; understanding of Latin American vs Peninsular variation; mastery of all subjunctive triggers including rare and literary uses
- **Italian**: full congiuntivo system (presente, passato, imperfetto, trapassato); period sentence structures in written Italian; stylistic awareness of Italian officialese vs colloquial registers

## Japanese (C1 / JLPT N1)

### Writing System Progress
- 2,000+ kanji — full coverage of the Jōyō kanji list
- Newspapers, novels, contracts, and academic papers fully accessible
- Kana abbreviations, archaic forms, and non-Jōyō kanji in specialist contexts

### Grammar Coverage
- Literary and archaic forms (である, されている, ものだ in formal prose)
- Four-character compounds (四字熟語): 一石二鳥、七転八起、etc. — fixed meaning chunks
- Keigo full production:
  - Sonkeigo (尊敬語): describing the listener's/third party's actions with respect
  - Kenjōgo (謙譲語): lowering oneself relative to the listener
  - Teineigo (丁寧語): polite neutral register mastered at B1
- Clause compression and nominalisation in academic Japanese
- Formal and archaic connectors: ~につき (due to), ~に際して (on the occasion of), ~をもって (by means of)
- Indirect and hedged expressions: ~かと思われます、~と思われる、~ではないでしょうか
- Advanced conditionals and hypotheticals: ~ともなると (when it comes to), ~ならではの (unique to)

### Sentence Structure
- Long bureaucratic sentences with multiple embeddings decoded naturally
- Full register awareness — spontaneous adjustment across casual, polite, and formal contexts
- Understanding of spoken compression (particle and verb dropping, contracted forms: ~てる for ~ている)

### Core Vocabulary Themes
- Academic and research vocabulary
- Law, politics, and bureaucracy
- Business, finance, and economics
- Classical references and proverbs (四字熟語)
- Colloquial slang and internet language (contrasts with formal register)

### Functional Skills
- Read national newspapers and academic papers
- Give formal presentations and speeches
- Write polished professional emails and reports in keigo
- Understand any broadcast media, film, and natural native conversation
- Participate in cultural and literary discussion

### Common Challenges
- Keigo full production — persistent challenge even for many highly advanced learners
- Classical Japanese forms (である, たる) in legal and literary contexts
- Understanding spoken spontaneous speech (extreme contraction, dialect, fast pace)

## Korean (C1 / TOPIK II-5/6)

### Writing System Progress
- Hangul fully automatic; Hanja recognition extends vocabulary inference significantly
- Academic and legal Korean accessible; literary Korean requires cultural context

### Grammar Coverage
- Complete formal and informal speech level switching — 존댓말 and 반말 mastered across all speech acts
- Written language formal structures: -(으)므로 (formal therefore), -는바 (given that), -(으)ㄹ지언정 (even if, concessive formal)
- Classical/archaic endings in legal and literary texts: -이다 (copula variants), -노라 (assertion)
- Advanced nominalisation and clause embedding in academic writing
- Full reported speech across tenses, moods, and speech levels
- Contrastive connectors at full depth: 반면에, 그럼에도 불구하고, 오히려
- Expressing nuanced modality: -(으)ㄹ 만하다 (worth doing), -기는커녕 (far from), -치고 (for/given)
- Discourse cohesion in long-form writing: 즉, 나아가, 이에 따라

### Register and Style
- Formal speech in professional contexts (business, academic presentations, job interviews)
- Written academic Korean style — highly Sino-Korean, clause-heavy, impersonal
- Colloquial internet Korean and youth slang (extreme contraction, loanwords)
- Awareness of North/South Korean vocabulary divergence in cultural context

### Core Vocabulary Themes
- Academic disciplines and research methodology
- Politics, law, and civic institutions
- Advanced science and technology
- Literature, philosophy, and cultural heritage
- Proverbs (속담) and four-character idioms (사자성어)

### Functional Skills
- Deliver polished academic and professional oral presentations
- Write structured academic essays and research summaries
- Understand native natural conversation including fast speech, dialect, and slang
- Navigate professional social situations with appropriate honorifics throughout
- Discuss literature, current affairs, and abstract ideas at depth

### Common Challenges
- Consistent honorific production under conversational pressure
- Academic written Korean (very different from spoken) — its own register to master
- Cultural depth: proverbs, historical references, and social norms embedded in language use

## Register and Speech Styles

At C1, register is no longer a set of rules to follow — it is a **creative and social tool**. Learners use register variation deliberately for rhetorical effect, humour, irony, and emphasis. They also handle unexpected register shifts from native speakers without losing the thread.

### Romance Languages
C1 learners command the **full register spectrum**, including registers they have never explicitly studied:

| Register | Description |
|---|---|
| Academic / literary | Subjonctif in all tenses, passé simple, complex clause structures — full passive use |
| Professional / journalistic | Nominalisations, impersonal constructions, formal connectors |
| Standard polite | Fluid tu/vous (or tú/usted) switching with full sociolinguistic awareness |
| Colloquial | Slang, contractions, ellipsis — used naturally and understood in context |
| Dialectal | Regional vocabulary and accent variation understood; cultural subtext grasped |

**C1 register as rhetorical tool**: Using *tu* with a stranger can signal warmth, aggression, or irony depending on tone — C1 learners read and deploy this. Switching from formal to informal mid-conversation for comic or emphatic effect is natural. Literary and archaic forms are understood in context (Proust, Cervantes, Dante).

### Japanese
C1 learners have **full keigo production** — the defining C1 milestone for Japanese:

| Register | When to use |
|---|---|
| Keigo — sonkeigo (尊敬語) | Customer service, speaking about/to executives, formal business — produced naturally |
| Keigo — kenjōgo (謙譲語) | Lowering yourself in formal business, customer-facing contexts |
| Teineigo (丁寧語) | General polite default |
| Plain form | Close social settings — natural and spontaneous |
| Casual spoken (compression) | Contracted forms (~てる for ~ている, particle dropping) — natural in fast casual speech |
| Formal written / literary | である, されている, ものだ — bureaucratic and literary contexts |

**C1 register challenge**: Maintaining keigo production *under conversational pressure* — i.e., when the conversation is fast, emotional, or topic-heavy. Many advanced learners can produce keigo in scripted settings but slip in spontaneous conversation. C1 is where this stabilises.

Four-character idioms (四字熟語) are deployed in speech and writing for stylistic effect — a mark of genuine cultural and linguistic sophistication.

### Korean
C1 learners achieve **seamless honorific switching** throughout a conversation as the social dynamic shifts:

| Register | When to use |
|---|---|
| 합쇼체 (-습니다) | Formal presentations, job interviews, official public contexts |
| 해요체 (-아요/-어요) | Everyday polite — most adult settings, default workplace |
| 해체 / 반말 | Close friends, peers, family, casual social settings |
| Academic written Korean | Formal essays, research writing — highly Sino-Korean, impersonal, clause-heavy |
| Internet / youth slang | Extreme contractions, loanwords, informal online communication — passively and actively understood |

**C1 register mastery**: Learners move fluidly between speech levels within a single conversation as roles shift — for example, speaking 합쇼체 during a formal presentation, then switching to 해요체 in the Q&A, then to 반말 with a close colleague in the hallway. This fluid real-time adjustment, without awkward hesitations, is the C1 benchmark.

Proverbs (속담) and four-character Sino-Korean idioms (사자성어) are understood and used in context, signalling cultural depth beyond grammatical competence.

## Comparative Overview

| Feature | Romance Languages | Japanese | Korean |
|------|------------------|----------|--------|
| Defining C1 Grammar | Full subjunctive in all tenses; stylistic register mastery | Full keigo production; formal and literary structures | Formal speech levels; advanced nominalisation and discourse |
| Script Challenge | None | Full Jōyō kanji (2,000+) | Hanja recognition aids vocabulary inference |
| Vocabulary Depth | Idiomatic, literary, and domain-specific | Classical compounds; 四字熟語; domain vocabulary | Sino-Korean vocabulary; academic 사자성어 |
| Register Mastery | Academic/literary/colloquial contrast | Formal keigo vs casual spoken contrast | Academic written vs spoken vs honorific |
| Authenticity Marker | Irony, humour, literary reference | Keigo under pressure; four-character idioms | Seamless honorific switching; proverb usage |

---

## Summary

- C1 is the point at which the learner stops translating internally and starts thinking in the target language
- For Romance languages, the primary C1 achievement is full stylistic and register flexibility — the grammar is known, the craft is the challenge
- For Japanese, active keigo production and full kanji coverage are the defining milestones
- For Korean, consistent honorific production across all social contexts and academic written fluency define C1
- At C1, authentic cultural content — literature, film, satire, formal debate — is fully accessible
- The gap between C1 and native-equivalent (C2) is primarily in depth of cultural and pragmatic knowledge, not grammar or vocabulary range
