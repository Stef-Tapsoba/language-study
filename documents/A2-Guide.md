# A2 Language Coverage Guide
*French, Spanish, Italian, Japanese, Korean*

---

## What A2 Means (Universal Definition)

At **A2 (Elementary)** level, a learner can:

- Describe past events and personal history
- Handle everyday situations — shopping, travel, appointments
- Communicate about immediate needs with some confidence
- Understand short, clear sentences on familiar topics
- Write short personal messages and notes
- Express simple opinions and preferences

A2 is focused on **daily life communication**, moving beyond survival into genuine independence on routine tasks.

---

## Romance Languages: French, Spanish, Italian

### Grammar Coverage
- Past tenses (passé composé / pretérito perfecto / passato prossimo — describing completed events)
- Imperfect (imparfait / imperfecto / imperfetto — background description, habitual past)
- Near future (futur proche / futuro próximo / futuro prossimo — plans and intentions)
- Reflexive verbs (daily routines: wake up, get dressed, etc.)
- Direct and indirect object pronouns
- Comparatives and superlatives
- Demonstratives (this / that / these / those)
- Basic relative clauses

### Core Vocabulary Themes
- Daily routines and household tasks
- Shopping, prices, and money
- Transport and travel
- Health and body
- Weather and seasons
- Emotions and feelings
- Hobbies and leisure

### Functional Skills
- Narrate what happened yesterday or last week
- Describe someone's appearance and personality
- Ask for and give directions
- Handle a transaction in a shop or restaurant
- Express and justify a simple preference
- Make and respond to invitations

### Language-Specific Notes
- **French**: distinction between passé composé (specific events) and imparfait (states/habits) is introduced here; learners often conflate them
- **Spanish**: both pretérito perfecto (recent past) and pretérito indefinido (completed past) are A2; region matters — Spain uses both, Latin America prefers indefinido
- **Italian**: modal verbs dovere/potere/volere become active (vs passive recognition at A1); futuro semplice introduced alongside prossimo

## Japanese (A2 / JLPT N4 Lower)

### Writing System Progress
- Full hiragana and katakana mastered (A1)
- ~100–200 kanji in reading (basic compounds: 学校, 電話, 食べ物)
- Kanji appear in example sentences; target-language explanations stay hiragana/katakana only

### Grammar Coverage
- Te-form and its uses: ongoing action (~ている), sequential (~てから), requests (~てください)
- Potential form: can/cannot (食べられる、行ける)
- Desire expression: ~たい (want to)
- Permission and prohibition: ~てもいい / ~てはいけない
- Giving and receiving verbs: あげる / もらう / くれる
- Basic conditionals: ~たら (if/when)
- Progressives: ~ている (habitual/ongoing)

### Sentence Structure
- Multi-clause sentences with て-form chains
- Topic-prominent constructions becoming natural
- Greater flexibility in particle usage

### Core Vocabulary Themes
- Work and school life
- Shopping and money
- Travel and transport
- Body and health
- Emotions and moods
- Seasons and activities

### Functional Skills
- Describe ongoing and habitual actions
- Express capability and incapability
- Give and follow multi-step instructions
- Talk about past experiences with some detail
- Ask for and grant permission

### Common Challenges
- Te-form conjugation rules (Group 1, 2, 3 verbs)
- Giving/receiving verb distinction (あげる vs くれる — perspective-dependent)
- Passé composé equivalent does not exist; aspect expressed through て-form

## Korean (A2 / TOPIK I-2)

### Writing System Progress
- Hangul fully mastered (A1)
- No new script; focus shifts to grammar and vocabulary expansion

### Grammar Coverage
- Near future: -(으)ㄹ 거예요 (plans and predictions)
- Desire: -고 싶다 (want to)
- Ability/inability: -(으)ㄹ 수 있다/없다 (can/cannot)
- Progressive: -고 있다 (currently doing)
- Sequential connector: -고 (and then)
- Causal connector: -아/어서 (so, because)
- Comparisons: 더 / 덜 / 가장 (more / less / most)

### Sentence Structure
- Connected clauses using -고, -아서, -지만
- Sentence-final endings expressing nuance (surprise, confirmation, mild assertion)
- Growing comfort with SOV word order in complex sentences

### Core Vocabulary Themes
- Daily routines and schedules
- Shopping and prices
- Transport and directions
- Health and body
- Hobbies and weekend activities
- Weather

### Functional Skills
- Describe current and ongoing actions
- Express wants, plans, and ability
- Connect ideas causally and sequentially
- Compare people and things
- Handle everyday service interactions

### Common Challenges
- Two connectors for "because": -아/어서 (objective cause) vs -니까 (subjective/persuasive) — distinction is A2/B1 boundary
- Aspect (ongoing vs completed) expressed grammatically, not through separate past/present tense variation
- Honorific speech level consistency in real interactions

## Register and Speech Styles

At A2, learners make their **first active register switch** — moving from always using the formal/polite form to deliberately choosing between formal and informal based on the social situation.

### Romance Languages
A2 learners move from *knowing* the tu/vous distinction to *actively applying* it. They practice framing the same sentence in both registers and begin reading social cues for when to switch.

| Register | When to use |
|---|---|
| Formal (*vous / usted / Lei*) | Strangers, professionals, elders, service contexts, anyone senior |
| Informal (*tu / tú / tu*) | Friends, peers, family, children, explicit mutual invitation |

**Key social rule**: Do not switch to informal unless invited. In French, Peninsular Spanish, and Italian business contexts, the formal register can be maintained even with long-term colleagues. Latin American Spanish tends toward informal more quickly.

**Casual/slang registers** are not yet productive at A2 — learners recognise common colloquialisms but do not produce them deliberately.

### Japanese
A2 learners become comfortable switching between **polite (です/ます)** and **plain form**:

| Register | When to use |
|---|---|
| Polite (です/ます) | Strangers, acquaintances, colleagues, teachers, service contexts — all default settings |
| Plain form | Close friends of similar or younger age; casual written messages; inside subordinate clauses |

**Key rule**: Plain form with close peers; です/ます with everyone else. Using plain form with a stranger or superior is a significant social misstep.

The giving/receiving verbs (あげる / もらう / くれる) introduce a new dimension: politeness has a *direction* — who is doing the favour for whom changes the verb choice.

### Korean
A2 learners begin **productive use of 반말** (informal speech, no -요 endings):

| Register | When to use |
|---|---|
| 해요체 (-요 endings) | Default polite — strangers, older people, new acquaintances, service contexts |
| 반말 (no -요) | Close friends of similar or younger age, younger siblings, children, clearly casual settings |
| 합쇼체 (-습니다) | Formal announcements, presentations, official speech — recognition only at A2 |

**Key social rule**: Never initiate 반말 — wait for the other person to set the informal tone or for the relationship to be clearly established. Getting this wrong reads as rude in Korean culture. When in doubt, keep the -요.

## Comparative Overview

| Feature | Romance Languages | Japanese | Korean |
|------|------------------|----------|--------|
| Main A2 Grammar Focus | Past tense contrast + pronouns | Te-form + potential | Near future + ability + connectors |
| Past Tense Type | Perfective + imperfective distinction | Aspect via て-form | Single past form + progressives |
| New Script Demands | None | ~100–200 new kanji | None (Hangul complete) |
| Biggest A2 Challenge | Tense selection (simple vs imperfect) | Te-form conjugation rules | Connector choice (-고 vs -아/어서 vs -니까) |
| Functional Leap | Narrating the past | Explaining processes | Expressing plans and ability |

---

## Summary

- A2 is where the past becomes expressible — learners can tell a story for the first time
- Romance languages introduce the critical perfective/imperfective distinction that trips up English speakers
- Japanese A2 is centred on the te-form, which unlocks a large portion of intermediate grammar
- Korean A2 consolidates the clause-connector system that defines the language's sentence architecture
- By the end of A2, learners should handle any routine daily interaction and narrate recent events
